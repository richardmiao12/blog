Electoral Prospectives for Peruvian President Amid Corruption Scandal




Corruption make the rounds on headlines in all countries once in a while, but in recent decades, it seems to really like the South American ones. Some could be quite lethal, like a drug-trafficking case by family members of Venezuela President Nicolás Maduro. Others could be more politically-oriented, like in Colombia, and Brazil, Brazil, Brazil.

Corruption charges, or even accusations, are generally seen as detrimental to the officials in question. A misuse of of public power for private gain could be seen by many voters as a betrayal. Yet, under some circumstances, this perceived betrayal may not be the definitive end of a political career.

Take the very recent Peruvian corruption scandal which has mired President Pedro Pablo Kuczynski. For his part in a 800,000$ business-bribery case, Kuczynski will face an imminent (occurring tomorrow, in fact) vote on his impeachment.

Contrary to this seemingly hopeless situation, filled with media outcry, pessimistic analysts, and worryingly low poll numbers, historical and recent research shows Kuczynski to have a fighting chance--- he likely won’t be successfully impeached, and could even win re-election four years down the road.

Do Voters Not Care About Corruption?

They do, and they show it. Unsurprisingly, following corruption scandals like our current case, there is generally a significant drop in opinion polls. While allegations of corruption (provided they aren’t extremely incendiary) won’t necessarily increase voter turnouts, those who do turnout to vote, as they normally do, tend to give the accused official a vote decrease of 6-11%.

A drop in opinion polls, however, could be attributed by multiple factors, even when corruption appears to be the main cause (meaning a drop in poll immediately following an exposé), existent dissatisfaction with the regime could strengthen the drop, while previously-strong faith in the official could help damp the decrease in popularity.

What Do Voters Care About More?

There are different degrees of severity in which voters would care about the public good an official has done, or proceed to the other extreme when charges are particularly outrageous. As a baseline, voters tend to punish officials more than supporting them through a potential redemption process. The tendency to punish an official despite their presentable public good holds true in Brazil, and could likely extend to other South American countries.

For smaller regions of rule (a small city of municipality), the public good aspect of an official tend to be more well-known, and therefore weighs more against potential punishments when corruption allegations emerge. For instance, in the municipality of pastrana, a town of 1200 citizens, the mayor broke regulation and allowed the apartment buildings to be constructed on rural land. Despite the suspicious nature of this action, and the Mayor’s observed likelihood to have taken a bribe of some kind, few residents took issue. The construction of these apartment buildings generated a considerable positive impact on the local economy. Dozens of construction projects provided employment and demands for local goods. Additionally, land-owning inhabitants saw profitable increase in their land values. This is a classic example of productivity trumping personal flaws in voters’ eyes.

On the other end of the stick is Alberto Fujimori, a former President of Peru involved in one of the most significant corruption events in the country’s history. While President, Fujimori was in fact placed under a great deal of control by his secret-police chief, Vladimiro Montesinos Torres. Montesinos accumulated years of bribery records (including written contracts and visual recordings) of influential officials within all branches of the government. Through Fujimori as a proxy, Montesino essentially ruled Peru for much of the 1990s as a puppet master. Public outrage at the President after this exposure was swift and furious. Former President Fujimori is still serving time in prison to this day. In fact, when Keiko Fujimori, current opposition leader and daughter of Alberto, was also accused of corruption last year, public outcry against her was perceivably harsher when compared to that of Kuczynski.




This reaction mismatch fits with research findings on the heterogeneity of corruption. Government officials whose corruptions result in some form of public compensation (like taking kickbacks but speeding a useful public-facility construction project), could be perceived as more forgivable than politicians with acts of corruption that actively harm the public (giving control of the government to the secret-police and undermining democracy). This difference in treatment is especially true for the Peruvian people, in context of the latter example’s extreme nature. Alberto Fujimori’s descendant is still suffering from the negative public image fallout, while her opposition may receive less punishment for his own corruption charges.

What Will Happen To Kuczynski?

Kuczynski is likely to avoid impeachment. While an impeachment seem assured numerically, with the opposition alliance and his own party members mustering a 71 out of 100 majority, there are external factors. The most important to note is Kuczynski’s expression last week of pardoning Alberto Fujimori, who is, once again, the father of the current opposition leader, and could technically stay in prison for another 19 years without a pardon.

Despite the lowering opinion poll numbers of Kuczynski, he would have until 2021 to turn the tide on his political career. This could be viable, as, in addition to his exhibited technical prowess, the economy has maintained an upward trend under his tenure.

At the end of the day, the fallout of an corruption exposure could incite great public negativity and, without good political maneuvers, be fatal to a political career. In the long-run, however, forgiveness is not out of the question for a competent politician who visibly improves the constituent’s welfare.
